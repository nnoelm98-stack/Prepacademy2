Verb Tense, Mood & Voice
Learn to keep tense consistent, choose the correct mood for hypothetical statements, and fix voice inconsistencies in parallel structures.
12 min read3 practice questions+20 pts on completion
The three verb properties the SAT tests
Tense places an action in time. Mood expresses whether a statement is fact, command, or hypothetical. Voice determines whether the subject acts or is acted upon.
TenseWhen to useExample
Simple presentGeneral truths, current statesScientists study climate data.
Simple pastCompleted past actionsThe team published in 2019.
Present perfectPast with present relevanceResearchers have documented a 40% decline.
Past perfectEarlier of two past eventsBy launch, the population had already declined.
Literary presentDiscussing texts, arguments, findingsDarwin argues that selection drives adaptation.
Subjunctive mood
Used for hypotheticals, wishes, recommendations. Triggers: if, wish, suggest, recommend, require, insist.
✓ "If the model were accurate…" (not "was")
✓ "The committee recommended she be appointed."
✓ "I wish the data were complete."
Active vs. passive voice
Both are grammatically correct. The SAT only tests passive when it breaks a parallel structure — mixing active and passive verbs in the same construction.
✗ Mixed: "Scientists discovered X and Y was identified by them."
✓ Parallel active: "Scientists discovered X and identified Y."
Two most common SAT traps
Trap 1 — Unnecessary tense shift: A past-tense passage suddenly uses present tense. Always match the established timeline.

Trap 2 — "Was" instead of "were" in subjunctive: Any contrary-to-fact conditional requires "were": "If the sample were larger…" / "If I were you…"
SAT strategy tips
Establish the base tense first
Read surrounding sentences. What tense does the passage use throughout? The answer must match unless there's a clear logical reason to shift.
Spot subjunctive triggers
The moment you see "if," "wish," "suggest," "recommend," or "require" — check: real condition or hypothetical? Real → indicative. Hypothetical → subjunctive ("were," "be").
Literary present for texts
When discussing a book, study, or theory, always use present tense: "Shakespeare depicts ambition as destructive" — even for centuries-old works.
Past perfect = "had + verb"
When two past events are described and one came first, the earlier one needs "had + past participle" to show the sequence clearly.
Practice questions
Passage
Adapted from a history article on the space race
1By the time Neil Armstrong set foot on the Moon in July 1969, NASA spent nearly a decade building the technical infrastructure, training the astronauts, and developing the computational systems that made the mission possible. The Apollo program drew on the work of over 400,000 engineers, scientists, and technicians across the United States.

6The mission's success marked a turning point not only in space exploration but in what humanity understood to be technologically achievable — a lesson that continued to shape ambitions for decades afterward.
Question 1 · Verb Tense
Which choice correctly replaces the underlined "spent"?
Aspent
Bhad spent
Chas spent
Dwas spending
Correct! "By the time Armstrong set foot on the Moon" signals that NASA's preparation happened before that past event. When one past action precedes another, the earlier one requires past perfect: "had spent." Simple past "spent" (A) fails to show the prior sequence. Present perfect "has spent" (C) implies ongoing relevance to the present. Past progressive "was spending" (D) implies simultaneous ongoing action, not completed preparation.
Not quite. Look at the time signal: "By the time Armstrong set foot." NASA's preparation happened before that moment. When one past event precedes another past event, which tense shows the earlier action? Think: "had + past participle."
Passage
Adapted from a philosophy essay on ethical decision-making
1Utilitarian ethics holds that an action is morally right if it produces the greatest good for the greatest number of people. Critics argue that it can justify troubling outcomes — for instance, that sacrificing one person to save five was morally required, provided the arithmetic of suffering favors it.

6This tension between aggregate welfare and individual rights remains one of the central unresolved problems in moral philosophy, with no consensus having emerged across two and a half centuries of debate.
Question 2 · Verb Tense
Which choice correctly replaces the underlined "was"?
Awas
Bwere
Cis
Dwould be
Correct! The passage uses present tense throughout: "holds," "argue," "remains." This clause reports what utilitarianism actually asserts as a current ethical position — it is not a contrary-to-fact hypothetical requiring subjunctive "were." Present tense "is" maintains consistency. "Was" (A) shifts to past without justification. "Were" (B) would be subjunctive — for contrary-to-fact only. "Would be" (D) adds unnecessary conditionality.
Not quite. Check the surrounding tense: "holds," "argue," "remains" — all present tense. This clause reports what utilitarianism currently claims. Which verb maintains tense consistency? And is this a genuine contrary-to-fact hypothetical requiring subjunctive "were," or a statement of what the theory actually asserts?
Passage
Adapted from a scientific article on coral reef restoration
1Marine biologists working on coral reef restoration have developed a promising technique: coral gardening. Fragments of healthy coral are attached to underwater structures, where they grow before being transplanted onto degraded reef sections. Early trials showed that survival rates improved when researchers carefully monitored water temperature and the fragments were given adequate time to establish before transplantation.

7The technique is now being scaled up at reef sites across the Caribbean and Indo-Pacific, with results that have exceeded early expectations.
Question 3 · Voice Consistency
Which choice corrects the underlined portion to maintain parallel voice?
Acarefully monitored water temperature and the fragments were given adequate time to establish
Bcarefully monitored water temperature and gave the fragments adequate time to establish
Cthe water temperature was carefully monitored and the fragments were given adequate time to establish
Dcarefully monitored water temperature and adequate time was given to the fragments to establish
Correct! The sentence opens: "when researchers carefully monitored water temperature" — active voice, subject = researchers. The second action must maintain parallel active voice: "researchers…gave the fragments adequate time." Option B keeps "researchers" as the active subject of both verbs: "monitored…and gave." Option A mixes active and passive. Option C shifts entirely to passive. Option D mixes active and passive again.
Not quite. The subject is "researchers" performing an action actively. Which option keeps "researchers" as the active subject of both verbs — "monitored AND [did something]" — rather than switching the second action to passive voice?
