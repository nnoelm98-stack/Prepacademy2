Parallel Structure
Learn to keep every element in a list, comparison, or paired construction in the same grammatical form — the principle that makes sentences feel balanced, clear, and correct.
12 min read3 practice questions+20 pts on completion
What parallel structure means
Parallel structure requires that items joined by coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or) or paired conjunctions (not only…but also, either…or, both…and) must be in the same grammatical form. If the first item is a noun, all items must be nouns. If it's an infinitive, all must be infinitives. If it's a gerund (-ing), all must be gerunds.
List parallelism
All items in a series must share the same grammatical form.
✗ "The program focuses on reading, writing, and to develop critical thinking."
✓ "The program focuses on reading, writing, and developing critical thinking."
Comparison parallelism
Both sides of a comparison must have matching grammatical structure.
✗ "She prefers hiking to when she swims."
✓ "She prefers hiking to swimming."
Paired conjunction parallelism
With not only…but also / either…or / both…and / neither…nor — the element after each conjunction must match.
✗ "The study was not only large but also it was rigorous."
✓ "The study was not only large but also rigorous."
Verb phrase parallelism
When two or more verb phrases are joined, each must have the same form — all infinitives, all gerunds, or all finite verbs.
✗ "The goal is to reduce costs, improving efficiency, and the elimination of waste."
✓ "The goal is to reduce costs, improve efficiency, and eliminate waste."
The four contexts where parallelism is tested
1
Lists of three or more items — the most common SAT pattern. The last item in a list is almost always where the error hides, since the first two items establish the pattern and the third breaks it.
✗ "The report evaluated costs, timelines, and what the staffing requirements were."
✓ "The report evaluated costs, timelines, and staffing requirements."
2
Paired conjunctions — whatever grammatical form follows the first conjunction must be repeated after the second. The SAT often places the error on the second element.
✗ "Either the committee will approve the plan or rejecting it outright."
✓ "Either the committee will approve the plan or reject it outright."
3
Comparisons with "than" or "as…as" — both sides of the comparison must be grammatically equivalent. A common error is comparing a noun to a clause or a gerund to an infinitive.
✗ "Composting is more effective than when you use chemical fertilizers."
✓ "Composting is more effective than using chemical fertilizers."
4
Clauses after "that" — when multiple clauses follow the same verb + "that," each clause must have the same structure. Dropping "that" before the second clause while keeping it before the first creates imbalance.
✗ "Researchers found that sleep deprivation reduces focus and productivity was impaired."
✓ "Researchers found that sleep deprivation reduces focus and that productivity was impaired."
The quick parallelism test
When you spot a list or paired construction, identify the grammatical form of the first item — noun, infinitive, gerund, adjective, clause — then check every other item against it. If any item breaks the pattern, it's wrong. Ask: could I list all items together with "and" between each pair and have them all sound natural? If not, the structure is broken.
SAT strategy tips
Find the first item first
The first item in any list or paired construction sets the pattern. Never change it — only fix the items that deviate from its form.
Label each item's form
Mentally tag each item: noun? infinitive? gerund? adjective? clause? If any tag differs from the others, that item needs to be rewritten to match.
Watch for "to" in infinitive lists
Once "to" is established before the first infinitive, subsequent infinitives can drop "to" (share the first one). But you cannot switch from infinitive to gerund mid-list.
Paired conjunctions demand exact symmetry
"Not only X but also Y" — X and Y must be the same form. Read what immediately follows each conjunction and compare them directly.
Practice questions
Passage
Adapted from a student essay on urban planning
1Successful urban transit systems share several characteristics: they are financially sustainable, politically supported, and the infrastructure is designed to be accessible to all riders regardless of income or ability. Cities that ignore any one of these factors tend to build systems that serve commuters in wealthy neighborhoods while leaving lower-income residents underserved.

6The most effective transit investments, researchers argue, treat mobility as a public good rather than a commercial service — a distinction that shapes every decision from route planning to fare structure.
Question 1 · Parallel Structure
Which choice most effectively corrects the underlined portion to maintain parallel structure?
Athe infrastructure is designed to be accessible to all riders regardless of income or ability
Bthey are accessible to all riders regardless of income or ability
Caccessibility for all riders regardless of income or ability
Ddesigning the infrastructure to be accessible to all riders regardless of income or ability
Correct! The list establishes a clear pattern: "they are financially sustainable, [they are] politically supported, and [they are] ___." Each item is an adjective predicate following "they are." The third item must also be an adjective predicate: "they are accessible to all riders." Option B correctly completes the parallel: "financially sustainable, politically supported, and accessible." Option A introduces a new subject ("the infrastructure") breaking the "they are" structure. Option C shifts to a noun phrase. Option D uses a gerund phrase that doesn't fit the predicate adjective pattern.
Not quite. Identify the pattern: "they are financially sustainable, [they are] politically supported, and [they are] ___." The first two items are adjective predicates following "they are." Which option completes the third item with another adjective predicate in the same form?
Passage
Adapted from a scientific article on ocean plastic pollution
1Marine biologists studying the effects of plastic pollution have found that microplastics not only accumulate in the tissues of fish and marine mammals but they also are entering the human food chain through seafood consumption. The implications for public health are still being studied, but preliminary data suggest a correlation between high seafood consumption and elevated levels of microplastic particles in blood and tissue samples.

7Researchers call for improved waste management systems, stricter regulations on single-use plastics, and expanded monitoring of microplastic levels in food supplies.
Question 2 · Parallel Structure
Which choice corrects the underlined portion to maintain parallel structure with the paired conjunction?
Abut they also are entering the human food chain through seafood consumption
Bbut also enter the human food chain through seafood consumption
Cbut also the human food chain is being entered through seafood consumption
Dbut also entering the human food chain through seafood consumption
Correct! The paired conjunction is "not only…but also." What follows "not only": "accumulate in the tissues." The verb form is the bare infinitive (base form) — "accumulate." The element after "but also" must match: "enter the human food chain." Option B correctly uses the parallel bare infinitive "enter." Option A introduces a new subject and auxiliary ("they also are entering") — broken parallel. Option C shifts to passive with a new subject. Option D uses the gerund "entering" — which doesn't match the base-form "accumulate."
Not quite. With "not only…but also," the grammatical form must match on both sides. What follows "not only"? "accumulate" — a bare infinitive (base form verb). What must follow "but also"? A bare infinitive in the same form. Which option uses a bare infinitive to match "accumulate"?
Passage
Adapted from a history essay on the printing press
1Gutenberg's printing press, introduced in the 1440s, fundamentally changed European society by making books cheaper to produce, distributing knowledge more widely, and the literacy rate across the continent was dramatically increased. Within decades of its introduction, the press had enabled the Reformation, accelerated the Renaissance, and contributed to the Scientific Revolution — all by making ideas reproducible and portable at a scale previously unimaginable.

7Few inventions in human history have matched its capacity to reshape the relationship between information and power.
Question 3 · Parallel Structure
Which choice corrects the underlined portion to maintain parallel structure?
Adistributing knowledge more widely, and the literacy rate across the continent was dramatically increased
Bdistributing knowledge more widely, and dramatically increasing literacy rates across the continent
Cdistributing knowledge more widely, and to dramatically increase literacy rates across the continent
Dto distribute knowledge more widely, and dramatically increasing literacy rates across the continent
Correct! The sentence lists three results of the printing press introduced with "by": "by making books cheaper, [by] distributing knowledge more widely, and [by] ___." The pattern is gerund (-ing form). All three must be gerunds. "Making" and "distributing" are already correct. The third item must also be a gerund: "dramatically increasing literacy rates." Option B correctly completes the parallel with "increasing." Option A uses a passive clause — broken parallel. Option C uses an infinitive "to increase." Option D breaks the first item from gerund to infinitive while inconsistently keeping "increasing" for the third.
Not quite. The sentence structure is "by [gerund 1], [gerund 2], and [gerund 3]." The preposition "by" establishes the pattern. "Making" and "distributing" are both gerunds (-ing form). Which option gives the third item the same -ing gerund form to complete the parallel?
