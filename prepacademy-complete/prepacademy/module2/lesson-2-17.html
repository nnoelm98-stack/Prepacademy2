Possessives & Apostrophes
Master every apostrophe rule — possessives for singular and plural nouns, contractions, and the possessive pronouns that never take apostrophes.
10 min read3 practice questions+20 pts on completion
The complete apostrophe ruleset
CaseRuleExample
Singular noun possessionAdd 's — always, even if the noun ends in sthe student's essay / the class's average / James's discovery
Plural noun ending in sAdd apostrophe only (after the s)the students' essays / the scientists' findings
Plural noun NOT ending in sAdd 'sthe children's books / the people's choice / the women's team
ContractionApostrophe replaces omitted lettersit's (it is/has) / they're (they are) / who's (who is/has) / don't (do not)
Possessive pronounsNEVER take apostrophesits / their / whose / your / our / hers / his / ours / theirs
The four pairs the SAT always confuses
its vs. it's
its = possessive pronoun (belonging to it). it's = contraction of "it is" or "it has." Test: can you substitute "it is"? If yes → it's. If no → its.
✓ "The study and its findings were clear." (possessive)
✓ "It's been proven that sleep matters." (it has)
their vs. they're vs. there
their = possessive (belonging to them). they're = they are. there = location or existential ("there is"). All sound identical — context is the only guide.
✓ "Their results were published." (possessive)
✓ "They're ready to present." (they are)
✓ "There are three approaches." (existential)
whose vs. who's
whose = possessive relative pronoun. who's = contraction of "who is" or "who has." Test: substitute "who is." If it works → who's. If not → whose.
✓ "The researcher whose paper was cited…" (possessive)
✓ "The scientist who's leading the team…" (who is)
your vs. you're
your = possessive (belonging to you). you're = contraction of "you are." Test: substitute "you are." If it works → you're. If not → your.
✓ "Your analysis was thorough." (possessive)
✓ "You're the lead author." (you are)
The ownership test for apostrophes in nouns
Before adding an apostrophe to a noun, ask: does this noun own something? Rephrase using "of": "the findings of the study" → "the study's findings" ✓. If the noun is just a simple plural with no ownership — "three studies were published" — no apostrophe. Plurals without possession never take apostrophes.
SAT strategy tips
Expand every contraction to test
For any word with an apostrophe, try expanding it: "it's" → "it is/has," "who's" → "who is/has," "they're" → "they are." If the expansion sounds wrong in context, you need the possessive form without an apostrophe.
Possessive pronouns never use apostrophes
Its, their, whose, your, our, hers, his, ours, theirs — none of these ever take an apostrophe. If you see "it's" used as a possessive, it's always wrong.
Check: is the noun plural or possessive?
Many SAT errors involve adding apostrophes to simple plurals. "The results shows…" → "results" is a plural, not a possessive — no apostrophe needed. Only add apostrophes when something belongs to the noun.
Plural possessive: find the plural first
For plural possessives, first form the plural, then add the apostrophe. Students → students' (apostrophe after the s). Children → children's (irregular plural, so add 's). Never add 's to an already-plural noun ending in s.
Practice questions
Passage
Adapted from a scientific article on migratory bird patterns
1Biologists studying long-distance bird migration have found that a species' ability to navigate thousands of miles with remarkable precision depends on multiple sensory systems working in concert. The birds use the position of the sun, the pattern of stars, and Earth's magnetic field — a redundancy that makes their navigation robust even when one system is disrupted.

6Recent research suggests that some species can also detect polarized light at dawn and dusk, giving them additional calibration points that help maintain accuracy across entire hemispheres.
Question 1 · Possessives
Is the apostrophe placement in "species'" correct, and why?
AYes — "species" is a plural noun ending in s, so the apostrophe correctly follows the s.
BNo — it should be "species's" because "species" is a singular noun.
CNo — it should be "specieses'" because a plural ending must be formed first.
DNo — possessive pronouns never take apostrophes, so "species'" is always wrong.
Correct! "Species" is a singular noun (it refers to one species). The singular possessive is formed by adding 's: "species's ability." However, many style guides — and the SAT — accept "species'" for singular nouns already ending in s, particularly for words where the extra syllable would be awkward. In this context, Option B is the most technically precise answer: "species" here is singular, and the possessive of a singular noun ending in s is "species's." Option A would be correct only if "species" were plural. Option C invents a non-word. Option D confuses nouns with pronouns.
Not quite. Is "species" singular or plural in this sentence? It refers to one type of bird — singular. For a singular noun, the possessive is formed with 's, not just an apostrophe after s. Which option identifies the correct form for a singular noun ending in s?
Passage
Adapted from a student essay on urban food deserts
1In many low-income urban neighborhoods, residents' access to fresh produce is severely limited by the absence of full-service grocery stores within walking distance. Corner stores and fast-food outlets fill the gap, but their offerings are typically high in sodium, sugar, and saturated fat — contributing to elevated rates of diet-related illness in the communities they're meant to serve.

6Policy solutions range from zoning incentives for grocery stores to subsidized farmers' markets, but implementation has been slow and uneven across cities.
Question 2 · Possessives & Contractions
The passage uses three underlined forms: "residents' access," "their," and "they're." Which assessment is correct?
AAll three are correct as written.
B"residents' access" should be "resident's access" because only one resident is implied.
C"their" should be "they're" because it refers to the stores' actions.
D"they're" should be "their" because it shows possession of the communities.
Correct! All three forms are correctly used. "Residents' access" — plural possessive (many residents share limited access), apostrophe after s ✓. "Their offerings" — possessive pronoun referring to corner stores and fast-food outlets, no apostrophe ✓. "They're meant to serve" — contraction of "they are" (the stores are meant to serve the communities) ✓. Option B is wrong — the passage clearly refers to multiple residents. Option C confuses possessive "their" with contraction "they're" — the stores' offerings belong to them, not a verb action. Option D reverses the error — "they're" here is indeed "they are," which is correct.
Not quite. Test each form: (1) "residents' access" — one resident or many? (2) "their offerings" — does this show possession or describe an action? Try expanding: "they are offerings" — does that work? (3) "they're meant to serve" — expand: "they are meant to serve" — does that work? Evaluate all three before choosing.
Passage
Adapted from a history essay on the Apollo program
1The engineers who's designs made the Moon landing possible came from extraordinarily diverse backgrounds — mathematicians, aeronautical engineers, former military pilots, and self-taught programmers among them. Many were women, particularly in the computing divisions, whose calculations provided the navigational precision the missions required.

6Despite their essential contributions, many of these engineers received little public recognition during their lifetimes, overshadowed by the astronauts who carried out the missions they designed.
Question 3 · Possessives
Which underlined word is used incorrectly, and what should it be?
A"who's" — should be "whose" (possessive, not contraction)
B"whose" — should be "who's" (contraction of "who has")
C"their" — should be "they're" (contraction of "they are")
DAll three are used correctly.
Correct! "Who's designs" — expand "who's": "who is designs" or "who has designs" — neither makes sense. The sentence needs the possessive: "whose designs." "Whose" (second use) — the women's calculations provided navigational precision. Expand "whose": "who is calculations" — nonsense. Possessive "whose" is correct ✓. "Their essential contributions" — possessive pronoun referring to the engineers' contributions ✓. The only error is the first "who's," which must be "whose" — a possessive relative pronoun showing that the designs belong to the engineers.
Not quite. Test each underlined word. For "who's" — expand it: "who is designs" or "who has designs." Does either make sense in the sentence? If not, you need the possessive "whose." Check the other two the same way.
