Commonly Confused Words
Master the word pairs that sound similar but mean different things — the last category of grammar errors the SAT tests, and one of the most reliable sources of points.
10 min read3 practice questions+20 pts on completion
The 12 most tested word pairs on the SAT
affect vs. effect
affect = verb (to influence). effect = noun (a result). Exception: "to effect change" = to bring about (rare verb use).
✓ "Stress affects sleep quality." / "The effect was immediate."
fewer vs. less
fewer = countable nouns (items you can count). less = uncountable nouns (amounts, qualities, abstractions).
✓ "Fewer studies / less research." / "Fewer hours / less time."
among vs. between
between = two items (or a precise relationship). among = three or more items (a general group).
✓ "Between the two options." / "Among the four candidates."
that vs. which
that = restrictive clauses (essential, no commas). which = nonrestrictive clauses (extra info, with commas).
✓ "The study that won the prize…" / "The study, which lasted five years,…"
imply vs. infer
imply = to suggest (the speaker/writer implies). infer = to conclude (the reader/listener infers).
✓ "The data implies a correlation." / "Readers can infer the cause."
amount vs. number
amount = uncountable quantities. number = countable items. Parallel to less/fewer.
✓ "A large amount of funding." / "A large number of studies."
comprise vs. compose
The whole comprises the parts. The parts compose the whole. Never "comprised of" — always "comprises."
✓ "The report comprises five sections." / "Five sections compose the report."
principle vs. principal
principle = a fundamental rule or belief (noun). principal = main/primary (adjective) or head of a school (noun).
✓ "A core principle of ethics." / "The principal investigator."
assure vs. ensure vs. insure
assure = reassure a person. ensure = guarantee an outcome. insure = financial insurance only.
✓ "She assured him it was safe." / "Steps to ensure accuracy."
since vs. because
since = from a point in time (or weak causal). because = the direct cause. Use "because" when stating an explicit reason.
✓ "Since 2010, rates have risen." / "Rates rose because of policy changes."
continual vs. continuous
continual = recurring with interruptions. continuous = uninterrupted, without pause.
✓ "Continual interruptions." / "A continuous data stream."
precede vs. proceed
precede = to come before. proceed = to continue forward.
✓ "Discovery precedes application." / "The team proceeded to publish."
Context substitution test
For any confused word pair, replace the underlined word with your candidate and read the full sentence. Ask: does the substitute carry the exact meaning the sentence requires? A word can "sound right" while meaning something subtly different. For affect/effect, ask: is this a verb describing an action (affect) or a noun describing a result (effect)?
SAT strategy tips
Affect/effect: default to noun "effect"
In the vast majority of SAT questions, "effect" is the noun and "affect" is the verb. If the word follows an article (the, an, a) or adjective — it's a noun → use "effect." If it follows a subject and means "to influence" — it's a verb → use "affect."
Fewer/less — the checkout lane test
If you can count individual items ("10 items or fewer"), use "fewer." If you measure a bulk amount ("less flour," "less time"), use "less." When in doubt, ask: can I count them one by one?
That vs. which — the comma test
If the clause has commas around it → use "which." If there are no commas and the clause is essential to identifying the noun → use "that." You can't use "that" after a comma — the SAT will test this.
Imply vs. infer — sender vs. receiver
The writer/speaker implies; the reader/listener infers. If the subject of the verb is a text, study, or speaker — use "imply." If the subject is a person drawing a conclusion from evidence — use "infer."
Practice questions
Passage
Adapted from a scientific article on antibiotic resistance
1The overuse of antibiotics has a profound affect on the evolution of bacterial populations. When antibiotics eliminate susceptible bacteria, resistant strains survive and reproduce — a process that accelerates when treatment courses are interrupted or when antibiotics are prescribed unnecessarily.

5Researchers emphasize that the principal challenge is not scientific but behavioral: persuading both physicians and patients to use antibiotics only when genuinely required.
Question 1 · Confused Words
Which underlined word is used incorrectly?
A"affect" — should be "effect" (a noun, not a verb, is needed here)
B"that" — should be "which" (the clause provides extra information)
C"principal" — should be "principle" (a fundamental rule, not "main")
DAll three are used correctly.
Correct! "A profound affect" — "affect" follows the indefinite article "a" and is used as a noun. But "affect" is almost always a verb. The noun form is "effect": "a profound effect on bacterial populations." ✓ "that accelerates" — the clause "that accelerates when…" is restrictive (it identifies which process), so "that" with no commas is correct ✓. "The principal challenge" — "principal" as an adjective means "main" or "primary," which is exactly the meaning needed here ✓. Only "affect" is wrong.
Not quite. Test each: (1) "a profound affect" — is a noun or verb needed after "a profound"? What part of speech follows an article? (2) "that accelerates" — is this clause restrictive or nonrestrictive? Does it have commas? (3) "the principal challenge" — does this mean "main challenge" or "a fundamental rule"?
Passage
Adapted from an economics essay on income inequality
1Economic mobility in the United States has declined over the past fifty years, with less Americans now able to surpass the income level of their parents than at any point since records began. The gap between the wealthiest and poorest households has widened considerably, and researchers infer that this trend will continue unless structural changes are made to education funding and tax policy.

6The data, which spans four decades of household income surveys, provides a comprehensive picture of how mobility has shifted across generations.
Question 2 · Confused Words
Which underlined word is used incorrectly?
A"less" — should be "fewer" (Americans are countable individuals)
B"infer" — should be "imply" (researchers are suggesting, not concluding)
C"which" — should be "that" (the clause is essential to identifying the data)
DAll three are used correctly.
Correct! "Less Americans" — Americans are countable individuals. The correct word for countable nouns is "fewer": "fewer Americans" ✓. "Researchers infer" — researchers are drawing a conclusion from the data they have analyzed, which is the definition of "infer" (to conclude from evidence). "Imply" would mean the researchers were merely suggesting without stating directly, but the sentence says they are concluding from the trend ✓ — actually "infer" is correct here. "Which spans" — the clause "which spans four decades" is set off by a comma and provides extra information about the data, making "which" correct ✓. Only "less" is wrong — it should be "fewer."
Not quite. Test each: (1) "less Americans" — can you count Americans individually? Countable → fewer or less? (2) "researchers infer" — are researchers drawing a conclusion from evidence, or are they suggesting something? (3) "which spans" — does this clause have a comma before it? Is it extra information or essential identification?
Passage
Adapted from a biology article on ecosystem resilience
1A healthy forest ecosystem comprises hundreds of interacting species, from the towering canopy trees to the microscopic fungi that form symbiotic networks in the soil. When one component is disrupted — by disease, invasive species, or human activity — the effects can cascade through the system in ways that are difficult to predict or ensure.

6Ecologists argue that maintaining biodiversity is the most reliable way to preserve a forest's capacity to absorb and recover from disturbance.
Question 3 · Confused Words
Which underlined word is used incorrectly?
A"comprises" — should be "is comprised of" (standard usage)
B"that" — should be "which" (the clause adds non-essential detail)
C"ensure" — should be "assure" (reassuring a person is intended)
D"ensure" — should be "reverse" or "control" (the sentence needs a word meaning to stop or manage the effects)
Correct! "Comprises" — the whole (a forest ecosystem) comprises the parts (hundreds of species). "Comprises" is correct; "comprised of" is considered an error in formal writing ✓. "That form symbiotic networks" — this clause is essential to identifying which specific fungi are meant (not just any fungi — the ones that form networks). No comma, restrictive → "that" is correct ✓. "Difficult to predict or ensure" — "ensure" means to guarantee an outcome. But the sentence says the effects are hard to predict or [stop/reverse/control]. "Ensure" doesn't mean to stop or manage something — it guarantees it. The correct word here would be something like "reverse" or "control." Option D correctly identifies this as the error and names an appropriate replacement.
Not quite. Test each: (1) "comprises" — does the whole comprise the parts, or do the parts comprise the whole? (2) "that form networks" — is this an essential identifying clause with no commas? (3) "difficult to predict or ensure" — what does "ensure" mean? Does that meaning fit here — can you "ensure" cascading effects, or do you want to stop/control them?
