Linear Equations — One Variable
Master solving equations with a single unknown — the foundation of all SAT algebra. Learn the step-by-step method and how to handle the three special cases.
12 min read3 practice questions+20 pts on completion
What is a linear equation in one variable?
A linear equation in one variable contains a single unknown (usually x) raised to the first power. The goal is always the same: isolate x on one side of the equation by performing the same operations on both sides.
ax + b = c
where a, b, and c are constants and x is the unknown. Solution: x = (c − b) / a
1
Distribute and simplify each side. Expand any parentheses. Combine like terms on each side separately before moving anything across the equals sign.
2
Move variable terms to one side. Add or subtract terms containing x so all x-terms are on the left. Add or subtract constants so all numbers are on the right.
3
Divide by the coefficient of x. Whatever number is multiplied by x — divide both sides by it. This isolates x and gives the solution.
4
Check by substituting back. Plug your answer into the original equation and verify both sides are equal. This catches arithmetic errors in under 10 seconds.
Worked example
Solve: 3(2x − 4) + 5 = 2x + 9

Step 1 — Distribute:   6x − 12 + 5 = 2x + 9  →  6x − 7 = 2x + 9
Step 2 — Move x-terms left:   6x − 2x − 7 = 9  →  4x − 7 = 9
Step 3 — Move constants right:   4x = 16
Step 4 — Divide:   x = 4
Check: 3(2·4 − 4) + 5 = 3(4) + 5 = 17  |  2(4) + 9 = 17 ✓
Three special cases
One solution (normal)
After solving, x equals a specific number. The equation is satisfied by exactly one value.
2x + 3 = 11 → x = 4 ✓
No solution
After simplifying, you get a false statement like 5 = 8. No value of x makes this true.
2x + 5 = 2x + 8 → 5 = 8 (impossible)
Infinitely many solutions
After simplifying, you get a true statement like 0 = 0 or 5 = 5. Every value of x satisfies the equation.
2(x + 3) = 2x + 6 → 6 = 6 (always true — all real numbers are solutions)
SAT strategy tips
Always distribute before moving terms
Don't move terms across the equals sign until you've fully expanded parentheses on each side. Moving before distributing causes the most common errors.
Watch for "no solution" traps
The SAT often writes equations that look solvable but produce a contradiction. If your x-terms cancel and leave a false statement — the answer is "no solution," not 0.
Fractions: multiply both sides by LCD first
If the equation has fractions, multiply every term by the least common denominator to clear them before solving. This prevents arithmetic errors.
Always verify with a check
On the SAT, substituting your answer back into the original equation takes 15 seconds and catches most errors. Always do it when time allows.

Problem
For what value of k does the following equation have no solution?
3(2x + k) = 6x − 15
Expand the left side and compare coefficients. A "no solution" equation produces a false constant statement after all variables cancel.
